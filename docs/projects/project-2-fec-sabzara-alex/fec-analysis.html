Blog post

We used the fec16 package, which contains data from the Federal Election Commission (FEC) for the 2015-2016 U.S. election cycle. We utilized the following datasets from the fec16.

Committees: Information on committees registered with the FEC, detailing their affiliations and financial activities.

Campaigns: Data on U.S. House and Senate campaigns, including candidate details and campaign expenditures. Results_house: Election results for the 2016 U.S. House of Representatives race, including vote counts and candidate information.

Contributions: A sample dataset of contributions from committees to candidates during the 2016 cycle.

Our analysis examined how money contributed by committees to candidates and spent in campaigns for or against candidates affected election results for the U.S. House of Representatives in New Jersey.

At first, we performed an inner join on the committees, contributions, and house results datasets. Our analysis revealed that winning candidates received $11,460 K from candidate committees. ( about two times more) while losing candidates received only about $ 5,400 K . The contributions to winning candidates were predominantly from Lobbyist/Registrant PACs (over two-third), followed by substantial funding from Unauthorized committees. In contrast, losing candidates had primarily relied on Unauthorized Committees followed by minimal amounts from Leadership PACs and Lobbyist/Registrant PACs.

In joining the sample dataset “contributions” with the full datasets of “campaigns” and “results_house”, and then filtering the results to New Jersey, We were able to find the contributions given to all candidates for the NJ House of Representatives and their results in the 2016 election. A pattern found by the dataset is that candidates that were elected often received multiple types of contributions. It is worth noting that one of the contribution types of this dataset also includes contributions that were made against those candidates. However, the candidates that were elected often had several different contribution types, so even if one was against the candidate, the others would outweigh it.

Our analysis shows that candidates who received higher contributions from committees were more likely to succeed in the 2016 House elections in New Jersey. A significant portion of these contributions came from lobbyist committees, suggesting they may influence elections by supporting preferred candidates. However, the data does not account for other factors, such as New Jersey’s Democratic-leaning political environment, which could partially explain the funding patterns. While the data shows a correlation between funding and election success, we cannot assume a direct cause. Other factors, such as the type of committee and the political affiliations of contributors, likely influenced the results. When joining data from different sources, it is crucial to consider the context and relationships between variables. Focusing solely on campaign contributions without acknowledging the broader political dynamics could lead to misleading conclusions. We must account for all variables and their interrelationships to avoid creating a misleading or incomplete picture.This highlights the importance of ethical data analysis: we must recognize the limitations of the datasets and avoid drawing oversimplified or false conclusions.
